# Task: Andrew Carnegie — The Steel King and the "Secret" Behind *Think and Grow Rich*

Source: *Think and Grow Rich!* by Napoleon Hill (Preface and threaded through every chapter — Carnegie is the originating figure of the entire book).

## 1. Background of the story
- Carnegie (1835–1919) — born in Dunfermline, Scotland; family emigrated to Allegheny, Pennsylvania in 1848 to escape poverty.
- Late-19th-century America: explosive demand for steel for railroads, bridges, and skyscrapers.
- Carnegie's place in *Think and Grow Rich*: he is the man who, more than 25 years before the book, "carelessly tossed" the secret of riches into young Napoleon Hill's mind and asked him to spend 20+ years organizing it into a philosophy anyone could follow.

## 2. Life path of the main role
- Arrival in America at age 13 — bobbin boy in a cotton mill at $1.20/week.
- Telegraph messenger; learns Morse code by ear so well he can transcribe without writing.
- Hired by the Pennsylvania Railroad; rises to division superintendent under Thomas A. Scott.
- Early investments in sleeping cars, oil, iron bridges, and telegraph lines under Scott's mentorship.
- Founds Carnegie Steel; vertically integrates ore, coke, transport, and mills.
- 1901: sells Carnegie Steel to J. P. Morgan for $480 million (becoming the largest part of the new U.S. Steel — the world's first billion-dollar corporation).
- Late life: gives away the bulk of his fortune — libraries, Carnegie Hall, the Carnegie Foundation, universities.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors
- **The Master Mind Principle.** Carnegie credited his fortune to a circle of about 50 associates ("partners") whose minds he coordinated toward a single aim. Hill cites this as one of the most powerful of all the 13 principles.
- **Definite Chief Aim.** A clear, written, dated goal — to dominate American steel — to which every decision was subordinated.
- **Specialized knowledge through other people.** Carnegie famously said he knew nothing about the manufacture of steel; he hired and motivated the men who did (Schwab, Frick, etc.).
- **Pleasing personality and ability to organize men.** His own epitaph: "Here lies one who knew how to gather around him men more clever than himself."
- **Going the extra mile.** Carnegie consistently delivered more and better than he was paid for — and taught his protégés to do the same.
- **Vertical integration** — controlling every cost-driver from ore in the ground to finished rails at the mill door.
- **Mentorship as a system.** Coaching young men (Schwab, Hill, etc.) in the formula multiplied his own thinking across many careers.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich
- One person's brain has a ceiling; a coordinated Master Mind of complementary minds does not.
- Wealth at scale comes from organizing other people's specialized knowledge, not from being the smartest specialist yourself.
- A clear, written, dated chief aim is the single most powerful tool — and the one most people skip.
- Develop a "pleasing personality" — Carnegie saw it as a hard skill, not a soft one, because it was the medium through which Master Mind power flowed.
- Build the system that compounds value — vertical integration in his era, network/data effects in ours.
- The endgame matters: Carnegie's reputation today rests as much on his giving as on his earning.
