# Task: R. U. Darby — "Three Feet from Gold" and the Million-Dollar Insurance Career

Source: *Think and Grow Rich!* by Napoleon Hill (Chapter 1: Desire / Chapter 8: Persistence).

Write `output.md` in this folder, covering the four sections below.

## 1. Background of the story
- Setting: the Colorado gold-rush era; R. U. Darby's uncle struck by "gold fever" and heading west to "DIG AND GROW RICH."
- The family context: Darby was a young man in Williamsburg, Maryland; his relatives and neighbors funded the mining venture on the strength of his word.
- Why Hill uses this story: it is the book's clearest illustration of how *quitting* — rather than lack of resources or talent — is what separates the rich from the rest.

## 2. Life path of the main role
Treat the story as a two-act life:
- **Act I — The Gold Mine.** The uncle stakes a claim, finds ore, retraces home for capital, returns with Darby and machinery. The first shipment proves it is one of the richest mines in Colorado. The vein then disappears. After failed drilling they sell the equipment to a junkman for a few hundred dollars and quit. The junkman hires a mining engineer who, on study of fault lines, drills *three feet* from where the Darbys stopped — and takes out millions.
- **Act II — Life Insurance.** Darby spends years repaying every dollar his relatives and neighbors had invested. He enters the life-insurance business with a single rule: "I stopped three feet from gold, but I will never stop because people say 'no' when I ask them to buy insurance." He becomes one of fewer than 50 people in America selling more than a million dollars of life insurance annually.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors
- The cost of quitting: the literal three feet that separated wealth from ruin.
- Turning a failure into a lifelong heuristic — every "no" reframed as "still drilling."
- Persistence as a *learned* skill, not an inborn trait.
- Picking a profession (insurance) where the failure mode is rejection, then deliberately raising his tolerance for rejection above competitors'.
- The compounding effect of "stickability": top-50-in-the-country status came from a single mental rule applied across thousands of sales calls.
- Seeking expert counsel (the junkman's lesson): the difference between knowledge and *organized* specialized knowledge.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich
- Most people are statistically much closer to success than they feel they are at the moment of quitting.
- Before abandoning a venture, get expert counsel and re-examine the assumptions — don't sell the machinery to the first junkman.
- Translate a single painful lesson into a permanent operating rule you can apply for the rest of your career.
- Choose a vocation whose main obstacle is rejection if you have already trained yourself not to quit — the field will overpay you.
- Pay your debts even when it takes years; reputation is the capital that funds the second act.
