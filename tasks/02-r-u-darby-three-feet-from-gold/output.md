# R. U. Darby — "Three Feet from Gold" and the Million-Dollar Insurance Career

## 1. Background of the story

The Colorado gold rush had pulled tens of thousands of men westward with the promise that anyone willing to swing a pick could "DIG AND GROW RICH." R. U. Darby's uncle was one of them. Back home in Williamsburg, Maryland, young Darby was the relative everybody trusted; when the uncle came east to raise money for the venture, neighbors and family wrote their checks on the strength of Darby's word.

Napoleon Hill returns to the Darby story repeatedly in *Think and Grow Rich* because it isolates a single variable. The Darbys were not lazy. They were not ignorant. They were not under-capitalized — at least not at the start. What separated them from a fortune was one decision: to quit. Hill uses the episode to argue that *quitting*, not lack of resources or talent, is what most reliably separates rich men from the rest.

## 2. Life path of the main role

### Act I — The Gold Mine

The uncle staked a claim in Colorado and began to dig. Ore appeared. He went back east for capital and machinery and returned with Darby and the equipment to work the vein at scale. The first shipment of ore proved the claim — by the assay, it was one of the richest mines in the state. A few more wagonloads and they would be out of debt; after that, every shovelful was profit.

Then the vein vanished. They drilled and they drilled, but the gold-bearing rock was no longer there. Their faith collapsed. They decided the claim had been a one-time accident and sold the machinery to a local junkman for "a few hundred dollars" — pennies on what it had cost — and went home defeated.

The junkman was not a quitter. He called in a mining engineer, who studied the geology of the site, mapped its fault lines, and concluded that the vein had simply jogged sideways at a known geological boundary. He instructed the junkman to drill exactly **three feet from gold** of where the Darbys had given up. The engineer was right. Millions of dollars in ore came out of that hole.

### Act II — Life Insurance

Darby went home, but he did not go quiet. He spent years repaying every dollar his relatives and neighbors had invested. Then he entered the life-insurance business, a profession whose daily currency is rejection. He carried into it a single private rule, born from the Colorado lesson and addressed to his own mind: *"I stopped three feet from gold, but I will never stop because people say 'no' when I ask them to buy insurance."*

That rule made him one of fewer than fifty Americans selling more than a million dollars of life insurance in a single year — back when a million dollars of premium volume was a genuine national distinction. The mine had been a closed door; the policies were an open one. The same man who lost a fortune in Colorado built a larger one in Maryland, on no machinery at all.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors

- **The literal cost of quitting.** The Darbys were three feet from gold, in physical, measurable distance. The story is striking precisely because the margin was so small. Hill argues that most quitters are similarly close — they just cannot see it from where they are standing.
- **Turning a single failure into a lifelong heuristic.** Most people learn nothing useful from defeat. Darby distilled his into one operating sentence and applied it to every future "no."
- **Persistence as a *learned* skill.** Hill insists persistence is not inborn temperament; it is a habit installed by a definite chief aim, a written plan, and an explicit decision not to be moved.
- **Choosing a profession whose main obstacle is rejection.** If you have trained yourself not to quit, you should pick a vocation where the bottleneck is rejection. The field will overpay you, because almost everyone else flinches at the second "no."
- **Compounding "stickability."** Top-50-in-America status was not built from a single heroic sale. It was thousands of routine calls, all governed by one mental rule, compounding for years.
- **Seeking expert counsel — the junkman's lesson.** The junkman did not "know more about mining" than the Darbys. He knew that he did not know, and he hired a mining engineer. Hill calls this the difference between knowledge and *organized* specialized knowledge.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich

The single most actionable line in the Darby story is the one Hill never quite writes but always implies: at the moment most people quit, they are statistically much closer to success than they feel. Discouragement is not a measurement; it is weather. Decisions made in bad weather are almost always wrong.

The protocol is straightforward:

1. **Before quitting any venture, get expert counsel.** Do not sell the machinery to the first junkman. The Darbys could have hired the mining engineer themselves for less than what they lost.
2. **Translate every painful lesson into a permanent operating rule.** Darby's rule was a single sentence he applied for the rest of his career. Yours can be too.
3. **Pick the field that punishes other people for the trait you have eliminated.** If you do not quit at "no," sell. If you do not quit at "failed launch," found startups. The rare temperament is the moat.
4. **Pay your debts even when it takes years.** Reputation is the capital that funds the second act. Without the repayment years, Darby could not have walked into Maryland boardrooms.
5. **Use the phrase as a checkpoint.** When you are tempted to abandon a project, ask out loud: *am I three feet from gold?*

### Lessons for getting rich today

- Before you quit anything, hire an expert and re-examine the assumptions — the Darbys' single conversation with a mining engineer was worth millions.
- Repeat to yourself, in your own words, "*I stopped three feet from gold, but I will never stop because people say 'no' when I ask them to buy insurance.*" Make it yours.
- Pick a vocation where the main obstacle is rejection; if you have trained yourself not to quit, you will get paid like Darby did.
- Pay back what you owe — financially, professionally, personally. Reputation is the working capital of every second act.
- Default to staying in the dig three more feet longer than feels reasonable. The reasonable distance is where everyone else stopped.

# 中文版本

# R. U. Darby —— "Three Feet from Gold"(离金子只差三英尺)与百万美元寿险事业

## 背景

科罗拉多淘金热曾把成千上万的人吸向西部,承诺只要肯挥镐,就能"DIG AND GROW RICH"(挖出富贵)。R. U. Darby 的叔父就是其中之一。回到 Maryland(马里兰州)Williamsburg(威廉斯堡)老家,年轻的 Darby 是亲戚都信任的那个人;叔父东归筹钱时,邻居和家人是凭 Darby 的一句话才签下支票的。

Napoleon Hill 在《Think and Grow Rich》中反复回到 Darby 的故事,因为它把一个单一变量孤立了出来。Darby 一家不懒。他们不无知。他们一开始也并非缺钱。把他们与一笔财富隔开的,只有一个决定:放弃。Hill 用这一段来论证 —— 真正最可靠地把富人和别人分开的,不是资源或天赋的缺失,而是*放弃*本身。

## 主角的人生轨迹

### 第一幕 —— 金矿

叔父在科罗拉多圈下矿权,开始挖。矿石出现了。他东归筹集资金和机器,带着 Darby 和设备一起回到矿场大规模开采。第一批矿石的化验结果证明了这处矿:它是州内最富的矿之一。再拉几车出来,他们就能还清债务;之后每一铲就都是利润。

然后矿脉消失了。他们一钻再钻,但含金的岩层不见了。信心崩塌。他们认定这处矿权只是一次性的意外,把机器以"几百美元"卖给了当地一个收废品的人 —— 远低于成本 —— 然后败兴而归。

收废品的人不是个放弃者。他请来一位采矿工程师,工程师研究了矿区地质,绘出断层走向,断定金脉在已知地质边界处只是横向偏移了。他指示收废品的人,在 Darby 一家放弃的地方再钻 —— 就在**离金子只差三英尺(three feet from gold)**的位置。工程师是对的。从那个洞里挖出了数百万美元的矿石。

### 第二幕 —— 寿险

Darby 回家了,但他并没有沉寂下去。他花了多年时间,把亲戚邻居投进去的每一块钱都还清。然后他进入了寿险业 —— 这是个日常货币就是被拒绝的行业。他带进这行的,是一条对自己的私人规则,生自科罗拉多的教训:*"我曾在离金子三英尺的地方停下,但我绝不会因为别人对我推销保险说'不'就停下来。"*("I stopped three feet from gold, but I will never stop because people say 'no' when I ask them to buy insurance.")

这条规则,让他成为全美少于五十位年保费销售额超过百万美元的代理人之一 —— 在那个年代,百万美元的保费量是真正全国性的成就。金矿是一扇关上的门,保单是一扇打开的门。那个在科罗拉多失去财富的人,在马里兰建起更大的财富,而且不需要任何机器。

## 致富之路与主要因素

- **放弃的字面成本。** Darby 一家离金子只差三英尺,是物理可测量的距离。这个故事之所以震撼,正是因为那条间距小到了可怜的程度。Hill 主张,大多数放弃者其实也一样接近 —— 只是从他们站的位置看不见。
- **把一次失败变成终身的启发式规则。** 大多数人从挫败中什么也学不到。Darby 把他的挫败提炼成一句操作语,应用到此后每一个"不"上。
- **坚持是一种*学得来*的能力。** Hill 坚持坚持不是天生气质,而是由明确的主要目标、书面计划,以及一个明确拒绝动摇的决定共同安装起来的习惯。
- **挑选一个主要障碍是被拒绝的职业。** 如果你已经训练自己不放弃,就去选那个瓶颈是被拒绝的职业。这个行业会给你超额报酬,因为几乎所有别人在第二个"不"那里就退缩了。
- **"黏性(stickability)"的复利效应。** 全美前五十的地位不是靠某一笔英雄式的销售建出来的,而是几千次例行拜访,全部由一条心理规则统御,在多年里复利出来。
- **寻求专家建议 —— 收废品人的教训。** 那位收废品的人并不"比 Darby 一家更懂采矿"。他知道自己不懂,所以请了一位采矿工程师。Hill 把这称作*知识*和*被组织起来的*专业知识之间的差别。

## 我们能学到什么 —— 尤其是关于致富

Darby 故事里最有可操作性的一句话,Hill 从未直接写出但始终暗示着:在大多数人放弃的那一刻,他们距离成功在统计意义上比他们感觉的近得多。沮丧并不是一个测量值,它是天气。在坏天气里做出的决定,几乎总是错的。

操作流程很直接:

1. **在放弃任何项目之前,先请教专家。** 不要把机器卖给第一个收废品的。Darby 一家本可以用比损失少得多的钱,自己雇那位采矿工程师。
2. **把每一次痛苦的教训翻译成永久的操作规则。** Darby 的规则是一句他终身使用的话。你也可以。
3. **挑那个会因你已消除的弱点而惩罚别人的行业。** 不在"不"前退缩,就去做销售。不在"发布失败"前退缩,就去创业。稀有的气质就是护城河。
4. **即便花上多年也要还清欠款。** 名声是为第二幕融资的资本。没有那段还债岁月,Darby 走不进 Maryland 的会议室。
5. **把这句话当作一个检查点。** 当你想放弃一个项目时,大声问自己:*我是不是离金子只差三英尺?*

### 今日致富的清单

- 在放弃任何事情之前,雇一位专家,重审你的假设 —— Darby 一家跟一位采矿工程师的一次对话,本可以值数百万。
- 用你自己的语言,把"*我曾在离金子三英尺的地方停下,但我绝不会因为别人对我推销保险说'不'就停下来*"重复给自己听。把它变成你的。
- 选一个主要障碍是被拒绝的职业;如果你已经训练自己不放弃,你会像 Darby 那样被支付高额回报。
- 还清你欠的 —— 钱、人情、信誉。名声是每一个第二幕的运营资本。
- 默认比"合理"距离再多挖三英尺。所谓"合理"的距离,就是别人停下来的地方。
