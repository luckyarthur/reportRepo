# Task: Dr. Frank W. Gunsaulus — The "Million-Dollar Sermon" and the Birth of Armour Institute

Source: *Think and Grow Rich!* by Napoleon Hill (Chapter 5: Imagination — "What I Would Do If I Had a Million Dollars").

## 1. Background of the story
- Setting: late-19th-century Chicago. Dr. Frank Wakeley Gunsaulus (1856–1921) was a young clergyman who began preaching in the stockyards region of South Chicago.
- His deepest desire was to head a new kind of college "in which young men and women would be taught to learn by doing" — without the constraints of orthodox education.
- The obstacle: he needed one million dollars in 19th-century dollars (roughly tens of millions today) and had no fortune of his own.

## 2. Life path of the main role
- Two years of vague, daily wishing: every night he took the thought to bed, every morning he picked it up again — "but he had done nothing but think."
- One Saturday afternoon he reaches a definite decision: he will raise the million dollars *within one week*. He has no idea how. The how is not his concern.
- He calls the newspapers and announces that the next morning he will preach a sermon titled "What I Would Do If I Had a Million Dollars."
- He writes the sermon that evening — easily, because he has been preparing it inside himself for nearly two years.
- The next morning he forgets the written notes at home. Standing in the pulpit, he closes his eyes and speaks from the heart.
- When he finishes, a man rises from the third row from the back, walks to the pulpit, and says: *"I liked your sermon. I believe you can do everything you said you would if you had a million dollars. If you will come to my office tomorrow morning, I will give you the million dollars. My name is Phillip D. Armour."*
- The money is delivered the next day. Gunsaulus founds the Armour Institute of Technology — which, after a 1940 merger, becomes today's Illinois Institute of Technology (IIT).
- Hill highlights: **the money arrived within 36 hours of the moment he reached a definite decision.**

## 3. How he became rich (in vision and influence) — main factors
- **Definite decision with a deadline.** "Within a week" turned two years of drift into 36 hours of result.
- **Definiteness of Purpose.** A vivid, specific picture of the college, the students, and what would be taught.
- **Burning desire** that had matured over two years — the readiness was real even though the action had been missing.
- **Imagination + Faith.** He could "see" himself already in possession of the million dollars before going to bed.
- **Auto-suggestion.** Daily incubation of the dream rewrote his subconscious until the request felt natural and confident.
- **Public commitment.** Calling the newspapers made retreat impossible.
- **Action congruent with the goal.** A clergyman *preached* — he used the only tool he had, but used it at its fullest.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich
- The single act of *deciding* — with a date attached — often unlocks resources that years of wishing cannot.
- You do not need to know "how" before you can decide on "what" and "when." The how shows up after the decision, not before.
- Use the medium you already command (your sermon, your code, your sales call) — don't wait until you've built a new one.
- Make the decision public; burn a small bridge. It compounds your commitment.
- Speak from a place of complete clarity about what you would *do* with the money, not what you would *get* from it. People fund visions, not requests.
