# Napoleon Hill — The Author's Own Life Path from Appalachian Cabin to Best-Selling Philosopher of Riches

## 1. Background of the story

Napoleon Hill (1883–1970) was born on October 26, 1883, **"in a two-room log cabin in the mountains of Wise County"** in southwest Virginia, the son of James Monroe Hill and Sara Sylvania Blair Hill. The phrase appears in Appendix A of *Think and Grow Rich* and it is meant to be taken straight: a log cabin, two rooms, in one of the poorest mountain counties in the United States.

His mother died when he was nine. His stepmother is the figure widely credited with civilizing his rough early temperament — pushing him toward education, toward writing, toward an outward life that the cabin alone would not have produced. By about age 13 Hill was writing for small mountain newspapers.

The defining moment of his career arrived around 1908, when, as a young magazine writer, he was assigned to interview Andrew Carnegie. Carnegie was already, by his own design, near the end of his earning life and well into his giving one (see folder `05`). At the close of the interview Carnegie issued a private challenge to Hill: spend more than twenty years organizing a "philosophy of personal achievement," based on the actual experience of the wealthiest and most successful Americans of the era. Carnegie would provide introductions but no salary. Hill accepted. The book the world would later read as *Think and Grow Rich* was, in effect, commissioned that afternoon — without payment, but with the largest possible commission letter.

## 2. Life path of the main role

- **Boyhood in Wise County, Virginia.** Two-room log cabin, mountain poverty, rough early temperament tempered by his stepmother.
- **Early writing.** Reporter for small mountain newspapers from about age 13. Eventually a journalist for several magazines.
- **The Carnegie interview, c. 1908.** Carnegie commissioned him to research and organize the principles of success by interviewing more than 500 of the wealthiest and most successful Americans of the era. The commission came with introductions but no salary — the apprenticeship was unpaid by design.
- **Twenty years of interviews.** Hill interviewed Edison, Ford, Schwab, Wrigley, John Wanamaker, Alexander Graham Bell, Cyrus H. K. Curtis, William Jennings Bryan, Theodore Roosevelt, and many more. He cross-referenced their habits, their decisions, and their stated principles into a single organized system. (Several of these figures have their own folders here — Edison in `04`, Carnegie in `05`, Schwab in `06`, Barnes-with-Edison in `01`.)
- **LaSalle Extension University.** Hill served as the school's **"advertising manager… when it was little more than a name,"** helping turn it into one of America's major correspondence-education institutions. The line, from Appendix A, gives a clear sense of how Hill spent his "in-between" years applying his own principles inside an actual business.
- **The Law of Success (1928).** The long-form result of the Carnegie commission. A multi-volume work that codified the 17 principles of success.
- **Think and Grow Rich! (1937).** The condensed, popular distillation — 13 principles, written for the working family in the depths of the Great Depression. It would become one of the best-selling self-help books of the twentieth century, selling tens of millions of copies in dozens of languages.
- **The Blair story.** Hill's own son, Blair, was born without normal hearing. Hill applied his own philosophy directly to Blair — desire, faith, autosuggestion — over nearly two decades. Blair ultimately did hear and speak. Hill writes about this in the book and treats it as his most personally credible testimony to the philosophy's power.
- **Later years.** Collaborated with insurance magnate W. Clement Stone; co-produced *Success Through a Positive Mental Attitude* (1960). Founded the Napoleon Hill Foundation, which continues to license and steward the books.
- **Death.** Hill died at age 87 on November 8, 1970, at his retirement home on Paris Mountain, near Greenville, South Carolina.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors

- **Definite chief aim with a 20-year time horizon.** The Carnegie commission was, in modern terms, a 20-year unpaid PhD in human achievement. Most people will not work two decades without a salary, even for the largest possible payoff at the end. Hill did.
- **Master Mind on an industrial scale.** Hill literally had a Master Mind of the 500 most successful Americans of his era feeding him primary research — including Edison, Ford, Carnegie, Schwab, and Wier. No other writer of the period had access to a comparable circle. The book is the organized output of that Master Mind.
- **Specialized knowledge organized for sale.** Hill converted his interview years into structured, packageable systems: the 17 principles of *The Law of Success*; the 13 principles of *Think and Grow Rich*; later the PMA (Positive Mental Attitude) framework with W. Clement Stone. Frameworks are sellable. Stories alone are not.
- **Persistence through staggering setbacks.** Hill suffered repeated financial disasters before *Think and Grow Rich* hit. He kept rebuilding plans — a direct application of the principle he had documented in James J. Hill's story (folder `10`): "*Temporary defeat is not permanent failure. Build other plans. Start over again.*"
- **The intellectual-property business model.** Books, magazines, lecture tours, correspondence courses, and licensing. A 20th-century equivalent of an info-product empire, with the manuscript as a compounding asset that paid royalties for decades.
- **A genuinely large market.** Everyone who wants to earn more, achieve more, and fear less is a potential reader. That is the largest possible addressable market a non-fiction author can choose. Hill chose it on purpose.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich

Hill's own life is, almost suspiciously, the cleanest possible test case for the principles in his own book. The most important lesson is the **20-year unpaid apprenticeship aimed at organizing a body of knowledge nobody else has bothered to organize.** Hill could have written one good magazine article a month for twenty years and lived a modestly successful career. Instead he spent those years doing primary research into a question — *how does anybody actually get rich?* — that no one else was systematically asking. The result was a manuscript that has paid royalties for nearly a century.

A second lesson is the **product-ization of expertise.** Hill did not just have interview notes. He had **organized systems** — numbered principles, named steps, decision rules. Frameworks are sellable; stories are not. If you have hard-won expertise, the wealth-creating move is to convert it into productized, scalable intellectual property — books, courses, software, frameworks — that earns while you sleep. Schwab created a corporation in ninety minutes (folder `06`); Hill created a royalty stream that lasted ninety years.

A third lesson is **find your Andrew Carnegie.** One mentor who can open doors to dozens of others is, by an enormous margin, the highest-leverage relationship available to anyone early in a career. Carnegie did not give Hill money; he gave Hill the introductions that produced a body of primary research nobody else had. Find the one figure who can introduce you to your industry's next thirty figures, and commit to a 10–20 year horizon with them.

A fourth lesson is **apply your own philosophy to yourself first.** Hill's most credible single testimony to the power of the principles was not Edison, not Ford, not Schwab — it was his son Blair, who learned to hear and speak under his father's deliberate application of desire, faith, and autosuggestion. If you are going to teach a method, the cheapest way to make yourself believable is to have visibly used it on yourself. Cross-reference Barnes (folder `01`) — Hill is, in his own life, the Barnes of his own book.

A fifth lesson is **the universal market.** A genuinely universal problem — how to earn more, achieve more, fear less — supports the largest possible audience and the longest possible royalty stream. Most authors choose niches because niches feel safer. Hill chose the broadest possible question on purpose, and the manuscript has out-earned virtually every contemporary niche book by orders of magnitude.

Finally — and this is rarely highlighted — Hill spent his "in-between" years as advertising manager of **LaSalle Extension University "when it was little more than a name."** He did not wait twenty years in a study. He spent the interim doing the unglamorous operational work of building an actual correspondence-education business — and what he learned about *marketing knowledge* in that role is visible on every page of *Think and Grow Rich*. The book is not the product of an ivory tower; it is the product of a working operator with a 20-year research agenda.

### Lessons for getting rich today

- The best apprenticeship of your life is often unpaid, deliberately chosen, and aimed at organizing a body of knowledge nobody else has bothered to organize. Plan for 10–20 years.
- Convert hard-won expertise into productized, scalable intellectual property — books, courses, software, frameworks — that earns while you sleep.
- Find your Andrew Carnegie. One mentor who introduces you to your industry's next thirty figures is the highest-leverage relationship available to anyone early in a career.
- Apply your own philosophy to yourself first. Hill's most credible testimony was his son Blair learning to hear. Yours should be just as visible.
- Choose a universal problem — how to earn more, achieve more, fear less. It supports the largest possible audience and the longest possible royalty stream.
- Do the unglamorous operational work in your "in-between" years. Hill ran LaSalle's marketing while writing the book; the operating experience showed.

# 中文版本

# Napoleon Hill(拿破仑·希尔)—— 作者本人:从阿巴拉契亚的小木屋到畅销的"致富哲学家"

## 背景

Napoleon Hill(1883–1970)生于 1883 年 10 月 26 日,**"在 Wise County 群山中的一间两间房的小木屋(in a two-room log cabin in the mountains of Wise County)"**,位于西南弗吉尼亚,是 James Monroe Hill 与 Sara Sylvania Blair Hill 的儿子。这句话出自《Think and Grow Rich》的 Appendix A,字面意思就是它写出来的样子:一间小木屋,两个房间,在美国最贫困的山区县之一。

母亲在他九岁时去世。继母被广泛认为是把他粗野的早年性格"驯化"过来的那个人 —— 是她推他走向教育、走向写作、走向那座小木屋本身不会产出的对外生活。大约 13 岁起,Hill 已经为山区的小报供稿。

他事业的决定性时刻发生在大约 1908 年。当时,作为年轻的杂志撰稿人,他被分配去采访 Andrew Carnegie(安德鲁·卡内基)。Carnegie 那时按他自己的设计,已临近"赚钱"生涯的尾段,深入到"散钱"那一段(见目录 `05`)。访谈结束时,Carnegie 向 Hill 提出一项私下挑战:用二十多年时间,基于那个时代最富有、最成功的美国人的真实经历,把一套"个人成就哲学"组织起来。Carnegie 会负责引荐,但不出工资。Hill 接受了。后来世人读到的那本《Think and Grow Rich》,实质上就是那个下午被托付下来的 —— 不付钱,但带着可能存在的最大委托函。

## 主角的人生轨迹

- **Wise County 的童年。** 两间房的小木屋、山区的贫困、被继母温和教化下来的粗野早年性格。
- **早年写作。** 大约 13 岁起为山区小报当记者。后来在几家杂志做记者。
- **Carnegie 访谈,约 1908 年。** Carnegie 委托他通过访谈那个时代 500 多位最富有、最成功的美国人,研究并整理出成功原则。委托附带引荐函,但没有工资 —— 这是一段被刻意设计成无薪的学徒期。
- **二十年的访谈。** Hill 访谈了 Edison(爱迪生)、Ford(福特)、Schwab(施瓦布)、Wrigley、John Wanamaker、Alexander Graham Bell(贝尔)、Cyrus H. K. Curtis、William Jennings Bryan、Theodore Roosevelt(老罗斯福)等等。他把他们的习惯、决定、说出口的原则,交叉对照,组织成一套系统。(其中几位在本仓库有自己的目录 —— Edison 在 `04`、Carnegie 在 `05`、Schwab 在 `06`、Barnes-与-Edison 在 `01`。)
- **LaSalle Extension University。** Hill 出任该校的**"广告经理(advertising manager),那时它'还几乎只是一个名字而已'(when it was little more than a name)"**,把它推上成为美国主要函授教育机构之一的轨道。这句话出自 Appendix A,清楚地写出 Hill 那段"中间岁月"是怎样在一家真实的企业内部,把他自己的原则用出来的。
- **The Law of Success(1928)。** Carnegie 委托的长篇成果。一部多卷本,把 17 条成功原则编码下来。
- **Think and Grow Rich!(1937)。** 浓缩、面向大众的精炼版 —— 13 条原则,写给大萧条最深处的工薪家庭。它最终成为二十世纪最畅销的自助类书籍之一,以多种语言售出几千万册。
- **Blair 的故事。** Hill 自己的儿子 Blair 出生时听力不正常。Hill 把自己的哲学直接用在 Blair 身上 —— 渴望、信念、自我暗示 —— 持续了近二十年。Blair 最终听到也说出了话。Hill 在书里写了这件事,并把它当作自己对这套哲学最具说服力的个人见证。
- **晚年。** 与保险业巨头 W. Clement Stone 合作,合著 *Success Through a Positive Mental Attitude*(1960)。创立 Napoleon Hill Foundation,至今仍在授权与守护他的著作。
- **辞世。** Hill 享年 87 岁,1970 年 11 月 8 日逝于他在南卡罗来纳州 Greenville(格林维尔)附近 Paris Mountain 的退休居所。

## 致富之路与主要因素

- **带有 20 年时间视野的明确主要目标。** Carnegie 的委托,按现代说法,是一份 20 年的人类成就学无薪 PhD。多数人不会在没有工资的前提下工作二十年,即便最终回报可能极大。Hill 做到了。
- **工业级规模的 Master Mind。** Hill 实际上拥有由那个时代 500 位最成功的美国人组成的 Master Mind,为他提供一手研究材料 —— 包括 Edison、Ford、Carnegie、Schwab、Wier。那个时代没有任何其他作者拥有可与之相比的圈子。这本书就是那个 Master Mind 被组织起来的产物。
- **被组织以备出售的专门知识。** Hill 把多年的访谈材料转化成结构化、可打包的系统:《Law of Success》的 17 条原则;《Think and Grow Rich》的 13 条原则;后来与 W. Clement Stone 合作的 PMA(积极心态)框架。框架是能卖的;只有故事不是。
- **熬过惊人挫败的坚持。** 在《Think and Grow Rich》大火之前,Hill 经历了反复的财务灾难。他一遍又一遍重做计划 —— 这正是他在 James J. Hill 那一段(目录 `10`)中记录下来的原则的直接应用:"*暂时性的失败不是永久性的失败。另起新计划,从头再来。*"
- **知识产权商业模式。** 书籍、杂志、演讲巡回、函授课程、授权。这是一个二十世纪版的"知识产品"帝国,其中那份手稿是一项几十年都持续派"版税"的复利资产。
- **真正巨大的市场。** 所有希望挣得更多、做到更多、害怕更少的人,都是潜在读者。这是任何非虚构作者所能选择的最大可能市场。Hill 是有意选的。

## 我们能学到什么 —— 尤其是关于致富

Hill 自己的一生,几乎可疑般地是他自己书里那些原则最干净的可能测试样本。最重要的一条教训是,**一段 20 年的无薪学徒期,有意瞄准整理一套别人没费心去整理过的知识体系**。Hill 大可以每个月写一篇过得去的杂志文章,过上二十年小有成就的日子。他却把这些年用来对一个问题做一手研究 —— *人究竟是怎么发财的?* —— 而别人都没在系统性地问。结果是一份手稿,版税付了近一百年。

第二条教训是**把专长产品化**。Hill 不仅有访谈笔记。他有**组织好的系统** —— 编号的原则、命名的步骤、决策规则。框架能卖;故事不能。如果你拥有得来不易的专长,创造财富的动作是把它转化成可产品化、可扩张的知识产权 —— 书、课程、软件、框架 —— 让它在你睡觉时也在赚钱。Schwab 在 90 分钟里造出了一家公司(目录 `06`);Hill 造出了一条持续 90 年的版税流。

第三条教训是**找到你的 Andrew Carnegie**。一位能把你引荐给几十位他人的导师,从杠杆比上看,几乎一定是任何人在职业生涯早期能拥有的最高杠杆关系。Carnegie 没给 Hill 钱;他给的是 Hill 那份别人都没拿到过的一手研究的入场券。找到那个能把你引荐给你所在行业接下来三十个关键人物的人,与他/她承诺一个 10 到 20 年的时间视野。

第四条教训是**先把你自己的哲学用在自己身上**。Hill 对这套原则力量最具说服力的单一见证,不是 Edison、不是 Ford、不是 Schwab —— 是他的儿子 Blair,在父亲对渴望、信念、自我暗示的有意应用之下,学会听与说。如果你要传授一套方法,让自己被相信最便宜的方式,就是看得见地把它用在自己身上。和 Barnes(目录 `01`)对照 —— Hill 在自己一生里,就是他自己那本书里的 Barnes。

第五条教训是**通用市场**。一个真正普世的问题 —— 如何挣得更多、做到更多、害怕更少 —— 支撑得起最大可能的受众,也支撑得起最长可能的版税流。多数作者选小众,因为小众"感觉更安全"。Hill 有意选了那个最广的问题,而这份手稿在销量上,把几乎所有同时代的小众书籍,按数量级甩在身后。

最后 —— 这一点几乎没人强调 —— Hill 把自己的"中间岁月"花在 **LaSalle Extension University"那时它'还几乎只是一个名字而已'"(when it was little more than a name)** 的广告经理位置上。他没有躲在书房里待二十年。他在那段时间里,实打实地为一家真实的函授教育公司做不光鲜的运营活 —— 而他在那个角色里关于"如何营销知识"所学到的,印在《Think and Grow Rich》的每一页上。这本书不是象牙塔的产物;它是一位手里有 20 年研究议程的实战运营者的产物。

### 今日致富的清单

- 你一生最好的学徒期往往是无薪的、被刻意选的,且瞄准整理一套别人没费心去整理过的知识体系。给它规划 10–20 年。
- 把得来不易的专长转化成可产品化、可扩张的知识产权 —— 书、课程、软件、框架 —— 让它在你睡觉时也在赚钱。
- 找到你的 Andrew Carnegie。一位能把你引荐给你所在行业接下来三十个关键人物的导师,是任何人在职业生涯早期能拥有的最高杠杆关系。
- 先把你自己的哲学用在自己身上。Hill 最具说服力的见证,是他的儿子 Blair 学会听。你的也该同样看得见。
- 选一个普世问题 —— 如何挣得更多、做到更多、害怕更少。它支撑得起最大的受众,也支撑得起最长的版税流。
- 在"中间岁月"里做不光鲜的运营活。Hill 在写书的同时管着 LaSalle 的营销;运营经验在书里看得见。
