# Task: Henry Ford — Decision, the V-8 Engine, and the Ford Fortune

Source: *Think and Grow Rich!* by Napoleon Hill (appearances in Chapters 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 — especially Chapter 7: Decision).

Write `output.md` in this folder covering the four sections below.

## 1. Background of the story
- Ford in the early-1900s American auto industry: a self-educated mechanic who did not reach high school.
- The state of the industry when the V-8 was conceived (mid-1930s): multi-cylinder engines existed but were cast as separate blocks and bolted together.
- The famous "ignorance" lawsuit anecdote Hill also tells — a Chicago newspaper called Ford "an ignorant pacifist"; on the witness stand Ford answered every "general knowledge" question by saying he had a row of electric push-buttons that could summon any expert in five minutes, and asked why he should clutter his mind with the answers.

## 2. Life path of the main role
- Working-class start, no formal higher education.
- Early temporary defeats — including more than one failed early automotive venture before the Ford Motor Company succeeded.
- The decision to mass-produce the Model T and build the assembly line; later, the leap from the four-cylinder to the V-8.
- The V-8 episode: Ford orders an eight-cylinder engine cast in a single block. Engineers unanimously declare it impossible. Ford's reply: "Produce it anyway." Six months — nothing. Another six months — nothing. Year-end check-in: "Go right ahead. I want it, and I'll have it." Soon after, the breakthrough comes "as if by a stroke of magic."
- Becoming one of the richest industrialists in American history.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors
- **Definiteness of Decision.** Ford did not negotiate with his engineers' verdict of "impossible"; he ruled it out as a permissible outcome.
- **Persistence backed by power.** He kept the order open for over a year without flinching.
- **The Master Mind principle.** Ford didn't need to know the answers — he organized a "Master Mind" of specialists (engineers, lawyers, accountants) and tapped their combined knowledge.
- **Specialized vs. general knowledge.** His push-button anecdote illustrates that *organized, applied* specialized knowledge — not encyclopedic recall — is what produces wealth.
- **A definite chief aim** (an affordable car for the masses) that organized every other decision.
- **Burning bridges.** Once decisions were made, Ford reversed them slowly and reluctantly — the opposite of the typical pattern Hill identifies in poor people.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich
- Reach decisions quickly, change them slowly. The opposite habit is one of the most common causes of failure.
- "Impossible" is usually a description of the current plan, not of the goal. Order the result; let the plan be discovered.
- You don't have to be the smartest person in the room — you have to *organize* the smartest people in the room.
- Formal schooling is not the same as ability to earn. Compensate with curated experts, continuous learning, and clear chief aims.
- Hold the line on the goal long enough for the breakthrough to become "obvious in retrospect."
