# Task: Stuart Austin Wier — The Engineer Who Re-Invented Himself as a Lawyer

Source: *Think and Grow Rich!* by Napoleon Hill (Author's Preface and Chapter 4: Specialized Knowledge).

Write `output.md` in this folder covering the four sections below.

## 1. Background of the story
- Setting: Dallas, Texas, in the years after Hill began circulating the "secret" of *Think and Grow Rich*.
- Stuart Austin Wier was a working engineer who encountered Hill's philosophy and decided his existing profession would not take him where he wanted to go.
- Hill cites Wier as one of his early test cases: "He was ready for it — so ready that he gave up his profession and studied law."

## 2. Life path of the main role
- Established career as an engineer — secure, technical, respectable, but not the path to the wealth and influence he wanted.
- Encounter with Hill's "secret": the realization that specialized knowledge becomes wealth only when *organized into a definite plan* aimed at money.
- Decision to abandon engineering mid-career and re-train as a lawyer — a deliberate transmutation of one body of specialized knowledge into another, more leverageable one.
- Builds a successful legal practice in Dallas, particularly serving the booming oil and gas industry where his engineering background gives him a rare combined competence (technical fluency + legal authority).
- Becomes one of Hill's recurring examples of how mid-life reinvention, properly executed, can produce more than a "safe" linear career.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors
- **Specialized knowledge organized for use.** Wier's engineering training was valuable, but it became *much more* valuable once paired with a law degree and aimed at a specific market.
- **Readiness.** Hill stresses the word: Wier was "ready" — i.e., desire, faith, and a definite chief aim were already in place when the principles arrived.
- **Decision over comfort.** Walking away from an established career took the same definiteness of decision Hill credits to Ford and Gunsaulus.
- **Hybrid expertise = scarcity.** Engineer-lawyers in early-20th-century Texas oil country were a tiny, premium category; rarity drives price.
- **Continuing education as a wealth strategy.** Specialized knowledge in *Think and Grow Rich* is not what you learned in school — it is what you acquire on purpose because it serves a definite chief aim.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich
- "Knowledge is power" is wrong as stated — *organized, applied* knowledge aimed at a definite chief aim is power.
- The fastest way to become rare (and therefore well paid) is to combine two existing skill stacks that the market rarely sees together.
- A mid-career pivot is not a failure of the first career — it is a deliberate upgrade of the toolkit.
- Pay tuition (time, money, late nights) for the specific knowledge your plan requires; do not over-invest in knowledge that has no place in the plan.
- Match your specialized knowledge to a booming industry whose participants will pay a premium for the combined skill.
