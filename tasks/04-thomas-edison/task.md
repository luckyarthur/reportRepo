# Task: Thomas A. Edison — Persistence Through 10,000 Failures

Source: *Think and Grow Rich!* by Napoleon Hill (Introduction, Chapter 1: Desire, Chapter 8: Persistence — Edison is referenced throughout the book).

Write `output.md` in this folder covering the four sections below.

## 1. Background of the story
- Edison (1847–1931) — limited formal schooling (a few months), partial deafness from childhood, sold newspapers on trains as a boy.
- The late-19th-century context: gas lighting was standard; a practical incandescent electric lamp was widely regarded as impossible. Edison also pursued the phonograph, motion pictures, the dictating machine, and an industrial R&D model itself.
- His role in *Think and Grow Rich* as the archetype of Persistence — Hill uses Edison's lab repeatedly as the proving ground for several of the 13 principles.

## 2. Life path of the main role
- Boyhood: hawking newspapers, conducting chemistry experiments in a baggage car, losing most of his hearing.
- Telegraph operator across multiple cities — first commercial successes were improvements to telegraph equipment.
- Menlo Park, NJ: building the world's first industrial research laboratory.
- The incandescent lamp: more than 10,000 experiments before a workable carbonized filament; Edison reportedly said each failure was "another way that won't work."
- Founding multiple companies (Edison General Electric, eventually a forerunner of GE), filing more than 1,000 patents in his lifetime.
- His hiring of Edwin C. Barnes and the launch of the dictating machine.

## 3. How he became rich — main factors
- **Persistence as a system, not a feeling.** Hill treats Edison's 10,000 failed filament experiments as the textbook case.
- **Definite chief aim.** Each project (lamp, phonograph, Ediphone) was stated as a concrete, physical objective.
- **Master Mind.** Edison surrounded himself with a team of "muckers" — chemists, mechanics, glassblowers, mathematicians — and is widely credited with inventing the industrial R&D lab itself.
- **Specialized knowledge organized for use** — Edison's lab subscribed to scientific journals worldwide and catalogued every experiment.
- **Imagination.** Reframing failure as data: "I have not failed. I have found 10,000 ways that won't work."
- **Owning the commercialization.** Edison kept the patents, licensed and manufactured the products himself, and built distribution — turning invention into income.

## 4. What we can learn — especially about getting rich
- Inventors who don't commercialize stay poor; Edison's wealth came from owning the *system* around the invention (patents, manufacturing, sales channels).
- Treat each "no" as expensive but informative — keep a written log of what didn't work, like an experimental notebook.
- The right team turns a single inventor into an institution. Hire the specialists whose knowledge you lack.
- Education credentials are not the bottleneck — applied, organized knowledge is. Edison left school early and out-earned almost all of his college-trained contemporaries.
- The "obvious in retrospect" inventions are usually the ones that someone stayed with for 10,000 iterations.
